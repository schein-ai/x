Why we started AI-native, from Vietnam, in 2025.

From the intersection of generative AI exploration and an established Vietnamese talent pipeline — how Tomoki and Nghia met, and how four businesses came to be.

Tomoki & Nghia Founder · Founding Partner
02 May 2026
Issue 009

xR&D Technologies was founded in 2025. We've published occasionally since — short notes, build logs, snippets from the inside. This is the long-form version: the founders' account of how the company started, what we got right, what we'd redo, and what we're still figuring out.

The premise was deceptively simple. An AI-native company built from two streams that didn't seem to belong together: a generative AI exploration running out of Tokyo, and a Vietnamese talent pipeline already years into its work in Da Nang. The company we ended up with is what happens when you commit to the intersection.

What follows is not a hagiography. We mis-priced the first three engagements. The "AI-native" workflow we were proudest of in pitch decks turned out to break under specific kinds of pressure we hadn't named yet. We've redrawn parts of the company on weekends, at airports, in conversations that started over coffee and ended over beer.

01 The convergenceTwo streams meeting in Da Nang.

By the time we met in early 2025, both of us had been working on adjacent halves of the same problem for years. Nghia had been building a Vietnamese talent pipeline in Da Nang — partner-university Japanese-language education, internship under live projects, graduate-time placement, repeated until the pipeline was a known supply line into Vietnamese IT firms.

That history matters. The pipeline isn't a recruiting funnel — it's a curriculum, paced over years, that produces engineers who use AI as a default rather than as a feature they bolted on later. Tomoki, meanwhile, was deep in generative AI prototyping in Tokyo, increasingly convinced that the interesting move was being on the building side of AI rather than just a user. Two years of separate work; one conversation.

02 The planWhat we wanted to build, and what we actually needed to build.

The first plan, sketched late at night in a notebook in Da Nang, was a single AI development studio. Two services. Vietnamese delivery, Japanese client side. We held an extended workshop with each other for a week in March 2025 and proposed something different.

The company we launched is four parallel businesses linked by a single AI-native foundation. Headcount roughly the same as a single-service shop would have been. Coverage cut across far more of what mid-sized JP enterprises actually need. The single studio became a four-business company.

  • Four businesses (development, visual production, adoption consulting, talent staffing), each with a clear scope but a shared technical foundation
  • One Da Nang HQ, a Hanoi sub-office, a Tokyo window for JP-side contracting
  • The talent pipeline as the supply side for all four businesses, not just staffing
  • A rule: everything we sell, we use ourselves first — the AI workflows we ship to clients are the ones we already run internally

03 What we got rightThe foundation, not the menu.

The single biggest decision in 2025 was treating the AI-native foundation as the primary thing the company is, not a feature of the services. Every business move begins there: the engineers we ship are pipeline-trained, the agents we build are tested on internal work first, the visuals we produce go through a QA pass we wrote for ourselves, and the consulting we sell is grounded in operations we already run.

Nine months in, the operating data tells us the foundation is working. Engagements ship inside the windows we estimate roughly most of the time, with quality bars that hold up to client review. Build speed is consistently 5–10× what equivalent traditional teams report. The pipeline keeps producing graduates we'd hire ourselves; the cohorts now feed into client engagements as well.

04 What we'd redrawThe bits we'd redo.

If we were starting again with what we know now, three things would change. We'd price the first three engagements differently. The current numbers feel right at conversation pace but undercounted the operational lift of having work spread across four businesses. A small tweak, big swing.

Second: the marketing rhythm should have been weekly from day one. We ran the calculations, decided weekly was thermally expensive in time, and now everything we publish has more friction than it needs. A rookie mistake — momentum is more than output.

Third — and we owe public write-ups on this — the four-business model needs better external explanation. We'll be publishing a clearer service map and pricing structure this summer. We'll publish it openly.

05 Detail studyThe talent pipeline.

The pipeline is the most-photographed thing about the company, and the most fought-over internally. Each cohort runs across years, integrates Japanese-language education with technical practice, and graduates engineers calibrated against three operating regimes: production-grade builds (closed, eval-tested), exploratory work (half-open, prompt-tuned), open contributions (open, paired with internal review for shipping).

The pipeline was a half-failure for years from a market perspective. Then we worked out we'd been positioning it wrong. The graduates sing in production.
Nghia · Founding Partner

The years-long curriculum was contentious. The market wanted faster bootcamps. We won the argument by ignoring the market and shipping a longer cohort. Two cohorts later, our graduates have rituals and instincts that bootcamp engineers spend years catching up to.

06 Detail studyThe AI-driven workflow.

The AI-driven workflow is the company's most boring document and its most consequential decision. Every role uses AI for code generation, every research thread uses AI for search and summarisation, every visual workstream uses AI for production. It costs about 11% more than the conventional setup once you count licences. It pays back faster than that, every quarter we measure.

08 What's nextThe second draft.

The company is operating. The first cohort of engagements has shipped or is shipping. We're still drafting — pricing v3, a new service map for the four-business model, a small office addition we're considering after a quiet conversation in Hanoi.

The Insights cycle will keep going. The cadence is now tuned to operational reality rather than launch milestones — we'll publish the first full year of operating data this autumn, on the company's first anniversary. After that: less often, but for as long as the work is interesting to write about.
