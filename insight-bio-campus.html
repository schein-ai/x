Eighteen months on a biophilic campus.

A retrospective from the Đà Nẵng research campus build log — what we got right, what we'd redraw, and the eight detail studies that made the climate work.

Lê Nguyễn & Mai Trần Principal · Design Director
28 Apr 2026
Issue 41

The Đà Nẵng research campus broke ground on October 12, 2024. We've published every two weeks since — site sections, structural decisions, the failed prototypes, the contractor arguments. This is the long-form version: 4,200 words on what eighteen months of construction taught us about designing in the tropics.

The brief was deceptively simple. A 7,800 m² campus for a research consortium of three Vietnamese universities, on a site that nobody else wanted: a former rice paddy at the edge of Đà Nẵng's industrial belt, hot, flat, exposed to the worst of the South China Sea's monsoons.

What follows is not a hagiography. The roof leaked in March 2025 and we owe a public apology to the contractor we blamed first. The screen detail we were proudest of in CAD turned out to deflect 14 mm under load — twice what we'd modelled. We redrew it on site over a weekend and a case of beer.

01 The siteAn old paddy, a new weather.

Đà Nẵng's climate is bimodal: a dry, fierce season from February through August, and a wet, fiercer one from September through January. Average rainfall in October exceeds 500 mm. The site sits 1.4 km from the coast on land that's been continuously cultivated for at least 600 years.

That history matters. The soil is heavy clay overlaid with two metres of organic alluvium, the kind that holds water like a sponge for a week and then releases it all in an afternoon. The neighbourhood — Hòa Vang ward — has flooded eleven times in the past century, the most recent in 2020. The campus had to sit above this without pretending the water wasn't coming.

02 The briefWhat three universities wanted, and what they actually needed.

The first design brief, written in late 2023, asked for a single 7,800 m² building. Three storeys. Air-conditioned throughout. A central atrium, naturally. We held a workshop with the consortium in February 2024 and proposed something different.

The campus we ended up with is four medium-sized pavilions linked by a covered colonnade, all under a single deeply-cantilevered roof. Floor area unchanged. Mechanical cooling cut by 62%. The atrium became a courtyard.

  • Four pavilions of 1,500–2,200 m² each, oriented within ±8° of true north–south for cross-ventilation
  • A 4.2 m roof overhang on the east and west façades, shading the entire glazed wall during peak solar hours
  • Operable timber screens on every south-facing opening, calibrated by season
  • A rain-buffer roof — 240 mm of growing medium and reclaimed terracotta — that holds 14 hours of rainfall before discharge

03 What we got rightThe roof as a brief, not a finish.

The single biggest decision on the project was treating the roof as the primary architectural instrument, not a closing gesture. Every climate move begins there: the overhang shades, the pitched plane sheds, the green stratum buffers, the photovoltaic strip generates, and the ridge channels operable cross-ventilation through every interior.

Twelve months in, the post-occupancy data tells us the climate strategy is working. Indoor operative temperatures have stayed within the 26–29 °C comfort band on 87% of occupied hours, including peak monsoon days. Lighting energy is 41% under the regional baseline. The 240 mm rain-buffer roof has discharged at the design rate every storm so far.

04 What we'd redrawThe bits we'd redo.

If we were starting again with what we know now, three things would change. We'd raise the colonnade roof another 600 mm. The current clearance feels right at human scale but creates a thermal stack that traps warm air at the higher ceiling junction. A small tweak, big swing.

Second: the lecture pavilion's east-facing window should have been operable. We ran the solar gain calculations, decided fixed glazing was thermally cheaper, and now the staff propose meeting in the colonnade because the room is "too still." A rookie mistake — comfort is more than thermal.

Third — and we owe drawings on this — the operable screen detail needs another iteration. Hannah, our project architect, is working on a v3 using a thinner timber sandwich panel that we'll prototype this summer. We'll publish it.

05 Detail studyThe operable screen.

The screens are the most-photographed thing on the project, and the most fought-over. Each panel is 2.4 × 1.1 m, weighs 38 kg, and folds back on a single brass pivot. There are 184 of them. They're calibrated against three seasonal regimes: monsoon (closed, drained), shoulder (half-open, breeze-tuned), dry (open, paired with operable louvres above for stack effect).

The screens were a half-failure for ten weeks. Then we worked out we'd modelled them dry. They sing in the wet.
Hannah Phạm · Project Architect

The brass pivot was contentious. The contractor wanted stainless. We won the argument by buying the brass ourselves and shipping it from Hà Nội. Eighteen months later, the pivots have a beautiful patina; the stainless ones we used as test pieces look exactly like they did the day they arrived.

06 Detail studyThe rain-buffer roof.

The growing roof is the campus's most boring drawing and its most consequential decision. 240 mm of free-draining substrate, a layer of reclaimed terracotta tile sized to the local roof tradition, and a planting palette of three local sedums and one creeping fig. It costs about 11% more than a conventional metal-deck roof. It pays back in five.

08 What's nextThe second draft.

The campus is occupied. The first cohort of students arrived in February. We're still drawing — operable screen v3, a new shading study for the western pavilion, a small pavilion addition that the consortium quietly asked us about over coffee in March.

The build log will keep going. The cycle is now tuned to occupied performance rather than construction milestones — we'll publish the first full year's energy and comfort data on the 12th of October, on the project's first anniversary. After that: less often, but for as long as the building is interesting to write about.
