Project No. 042

An elevated promenade that doubles as a city's flood defence.

The Mekong Delta is one of the most climate-exposed regions in Southeast Asia — by 2050, half of Cần Thơ's footprint sits inside the once-a-decade flood line. The province asked us a deceptively simple question: can civic space and flood resilience share the same budget?

The Resilience Loop is our answer. A 14.2 km elevated walkway that traces the riverfront, lifted above the design-level flood line, with a porous understory of stilted basins, retention gardens, and shaded markets that absorb water during peak monsoon and release it gradually downstream.

Built in three stages between 2021 and 2024, the loop is now used by an estimated 180,000 people each week and absorbs the equivalent of 41 Olympic pools of stormwater per major event.

The premise

A river that rises, a city that learns to rise with it.

For most of the 20th century, delta cities defended themselves with concrete: dykes, sea walls, sluice gates. The trouble is that a hard edge fails hard. When it overtops, everything behind it floods at once — and the more we build, the higher the next wall has to be.

Cần Thơ wanted a different posture. Not a wall, but a layered surface — one that lets water in slowly, holds it, releases it, and gives the city something useful (a riverwalk, a market, a shaded place to gather) when the water isn't there.

We worked with hydrologists at Đại học Cần Thơ on a generative model that simulated 80 years of rainfall, tide, and upstream-dam release scenarios. The model didn't design the loop — but it told us where to put the basins, how high to lift the deck, and how wide the understory had to be.

The approach

Four moves, one continuous deck.

The loop is a single 14.2 km gesture, but it does four jobs. Each move was simulated, prototyped at 1:1 on a 80m test stretch in Bình Thuỷ ward, and then rolled out across the full length.

Move 01

Lift the deck

The walkway sits 2.4 m above the design-flood line — enough to stay dry under the worst event in the 80-year simulation, low enough to feel like a street rather than a viaduct.

Move 02

Open the understory

Below the deck: a 12 m-wide porous landscape of stilted basins, raingardens, and shaded market plots. It absorbs storm runoff before the river even crests.

Move 03

Stitch the cross-streets

Eight transverse ramps reconnect inland neighborhoods to the river. The previous embankment had cut them off for 60 years.

Move 04

Plant the edge

The river-facing edge is a 4 m mangrove revival strip — replacing a hardened seawall with a softer, self-rebuilding edge that's already trapping sediment.
