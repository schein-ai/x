One studio, four businesses: 3 synergy patterns.

Build × visual, consulting → build, build + talent. The cross-business engagements clients keep coming back for, and why the four sit better together than apart.

Tomoki Founder · CEO
15 Apr 2026
Issue 006

When we drew xR&D as four parallel businesses on a whiteboard in Da Nang, the first reaction from advisors was reasonable: pick one. Two at most. Four would be hard to position, hard to staff, hard to sell. Nine months in, the four-business model is the most consistent reason clients come back. Here are the three engagement patterns we keep seeing.

None of these were planned. Each emerged from a single client engagement, then repeated until we noticed the pattern. The order — build, then visual, then consulting, then talent — is roughly the order they appear in a typical client relationship.

01 The fourWhat we actually sell.

Briefly, for context: build is AI-driven application development. Visual is AI-native creative production — campaign visuals, brand systems, motion. Consulting is AI adoption advisory for mid-sized JP enterprises. Talent is staffing AI-native engineers into client teams.

Each of the four can run as a standalone engagement and roughly half of our work does. The other half follows one of three cross-business patterns described below.

02 Pattern oneBuild × Visual.

The most common pattern. A client commissions an AI agent or application from the build team. Three weeks in, they realise the launch needs visual assets — onboarding screens, marketing campaign, internal training material. Visual takes the brief from build directly. Same studio, same context, no rebriefing.

The reason it works: the visual team has been watching the build from inside, so they understand the product they're rendering. The AI-native foundation is shared — the prompts, the QA pass, the production rituals. A client who would otherwise pay a build team and a separate creative agency gets a single coherent output for less than the sum.

03 Pattern twoConsulting → Build.

The slower-burn pattern. A mid-sized JP enterprise engages the consulting team to map their AI adoption roadmap. The output is a strategy document — sometimes that's the entire engagement. About 40% of the time, the client follows up with a build phase: take one of the recommendations and ship it. The build team picks up where consulting left off, with full context.

The first version of the consulting deliverable was a deck. The second version was a deck plus the team that would actually ship the recommendation. The second version sells.

This pattern is the strategic answer to the consulting industry's perennial problem: recommendations that don't land. We sell the recommendation alongside the team that will execute it. The handover from consulting to build takes a week, not the typical three months.

04 Pattern threeBuild + Talent.

The third pattern is the inverse of consulting → build. A client begins with a build engagement; partway through, they realise they need permanent AI capability inside their own team. The talent team places one or two of our engineers into the client's organisation, on terms that vary from short-term embed to permanent transfer.

This pattern is delicate. We're effectively giving up the engineer. The economics work because the placed engineer carries the relationship — the build team continues to ship for the client, with the placed engineer as the inside connection. We've placed seventeen engineers this way in the first nine months. Retention is high; relationships compound.

05 Why they connectThe shared foundation.

The reason the patterns work isn't accident. It's the shared AI-native foundation. Every business uses the same tooling, the same review rituals, the same engineers (sometimes literally the same engineers). A handover between businesses is a handover within a single operating frame. There is no translation cost.

  • One operating standard. Code review, visual QA, consulting deliverables — all share the same AI-native review pass
  • One talent supply. Engineers from the pipeline rotate across business lines; visual designers learn build vocabulary; consultants come from build backgrounds
  • One client conversation. A client speaks to one studio, even when the work spans three of the four businesses

06 What we'd watchThe tradeoffs.

Four businesses is harder to staff than one. It's also harder to position publicly — the elevator pitch is genuinely longer. We accept both costs because the pattern data tells us they're worth it. If the cross-business engagements were 10% of revenue, we'd cut to two businesses tomorrow. They're closer to 50% and growing, so we don't.

The thing we watch most carefully is the load on individual engineers. Pipeline graduates often work across business lines in their first year. That's a feature for them — they get range — but it has to be paced. We've started capping cross-business assignments at two simultaneous engagements per engineer.

07 What's nextA fifth?

People ask if there's a fifth business coming. Right now, no. The four are still settling. The pipeline is calibrated to four; the operations team is calibrated to four; the marketing rhythm is barely keeping up with four. A fifth would dilute the operating standard.

If the four-business model holds for another year and the cross-business patterns keep compounding, we'll write a longer piece on what a fifth could look like. Until then: four, well-run, with the synergies above doing real work.
