How AI-driven workflows save us 3 days a week.

Code, visuals, research, testing — every part of our process runs through AI as the default. A look at what gets used, where it fails, and what's still manual.

Engineering xR&D Build · Da Nang
05 Apr 2026
Issue 004

Every quarter we tally up internal time. The latest tally puts AI-driven workflows at roughly three working days saved per engineer per week, against a counterfactual of the same work done without AI assistance. The number is rough — there's no perfect counterfactual — but it's been stable for three quarters in a row, so we're publishing it.

This is a method note, not a celebration. The interesting part is where the saving comes from, where it doesn't, and what we still do manually because the AI can't yet help.

01 Where it savesThe boring wins.

Roughly half of the saving is from boring tasks: meeting notes, code review setup, email drafts in Japanese, ticket triage, status updates. None of these were ever the interesting part of an engineer's day. AI eats them, the engineer never does them, the day starts with the actual work.

  • Meeting notes — automatic transcription + summary, reviewed in 90 seconds
  • Code review — first pass run by AI before human review (catches roughly 40% of issues)
  • Status writeups — drafted from commit history and slack, edited by human
  • JP-language drafts — bilingual engineers no longer write client emails from scratch
  • Ticket triage — auto-categorised, auto-prioritised, human-confirmed

02 Where it saves moreThe interesting wins.

The other half is from work that used to be interesting but is now routine: scaffolding code, writing tests, generating visual variants, drafting prompts for downstream agents. These are the wins that change what an engineer does with their week — fewer hours on plumbing, more on the parts that need human judgement.

For a typical build engineer, that's roughly 15 hours a week reclaimed from plumbing into design and review. The 15 hours don't all go back into "more output" — some of it goes into deeper review, some into reading, some into pair time with newer engineers. We don't optimise for output throughput; we optimise for output quality.

03 Where it failsThree known failure modes.

Failure one: domain depth. The AI is fluent on common patterns and shallow on uncommon ones. For a senior engineer working on a niche manufacturing protocol, the assistant slows them down more often than it speeds them up. We've stopped pushing AI use in those contexts and let the engineer call when they want it.

The AI is great at the first 80% of any new task. The last 20% is where humans still earn their keep, and the assistant gets in the way of that part.
Engineering lead · Build

Failure two: confident wrongness. The model writes a plausible-sounding answer that is wrong in subtle ways. We catch most of these in review, but the catch-rate isn't 100%. We've added an explicit "model confidence" check on critical outputs — if the model can't justify its answer, the task escalates to human review.

Failure three: over-trust by junior engineers. New engineers are sometimes too willing to accept AI output as correct. The pipeline curriculum addresses this directly — year two is largely about teaching engineers to challenge model outputs reliably. It works, but it takes deliberate practice.

04 What's still manualWhere AI doesn't help us.

Three categories of work where AI use is intentionally minimal:

  • Client conversations. No AI in live calls. We take notes after, we don't transcribe-and-summarise during. The conversation matters more than the artefact.
  • Architecture decisions. AI can scaffold. AI doesn't get to choose the architecture. Senior engineers do that on whiteboards, with humans only.
  • Hiring and 1:1s. Internal HR work is fully manual. Trust is built between humans, period.

05 The costWhat this actually costs.

The full AI-tooling stack costs about USD 380 per engineer per month at current pricing — codegen, image generation, document Q&A, transcription, the agent tooling we use ourselves. That's roughly 11% of total per-engineer operating cost. The three days a week paid for itself within the first month of any engineer's tenure.

This number will change. Tooling pricing is volatile; we re-tally each quarter. We don't anchor commercial decisions on the savings — they're a margin compounding effect, not the basis of pricing.

06 What we'll changeNext quarter.

Two changes coming in Q3:

  • Internal "AI-no-go" list. An explicit list of contexts where the team is told not to use AI (currently informal). Formalising it removes ambiguity.
  • Quarterly retro on the workflow itself. We've been reviewing engagements but not the meta-workflow. Adding that as a standing review.

The full operating notes ship as part of the quarterly dossier, available to clients on request. The headline numbers will continue to publish here.
