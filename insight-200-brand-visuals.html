Generating 200 brand visuals in five days.

Multi-language, multi-variation campaign visuals for a JP consumer goods launch — without a photo shoot. The prompt scaffolding, the QA pass, and the output we shipped.

Creative xR&D Visual · Da Nang
30 Mar 2026
Issue 003

A JP consumer goods client booked us on a Monday morning to ship 200 campaign visuals by Friday end-of-day. The brief: four product variants, five regional adaptations, ten size formats, two languages. No photo shoot, no agency overhead, full creative direction. We delivered 218 visuals at 16:40 on the Friday. Here's what the week looked like.

01 The briefWhat the client needed.

The launch was a new product line aimed at four customer segments, with regional adaptation for five JP markets and two languages (JP / EN). Each segment needed a hero visual, three lifestyle scenes, two product detail crops. Multiplied across formats and locales, the math comes out to ~200 distinct outputs.

Conventional approach: hire a photo studio, book talent, two weeks of pre-production, three days of shoot, two weeks of post. Six-figure budget, six-week timeline. The client had four weeks until launch and a fraction of that budget. They came to us asking if AI could close the gap.

02 The setupDay one.

Monday morning was scoping. By Monday afternoon we had a creative direction agreed, a brand-style reference deck, a list of regional adaptations, and a prompt scaffolding plan. Two designers, one prompt engineer, one client-side reviewer. The team stayed at four people through Friday.

  • Reference pack — 40 images from prior brand work, used as style anchors
  • Prompt scaffolding — modular prompt templates (subject, lighting, locale, language)
  • Generation pipeline — base model with brand-specific LoRA fine-tune we already had
  • Review loop — human approval gate every 50 images, structured rubric

03 The middle daysTuesday through Thursday.

Tuesday and Wednesday were generation. Roughly 1,800 candidates went through the pipeline. About 11% passed the first-pass automated QA (composition, brand alignment, colour). The remaining 89% went into a triage bucket — some salvageable with prompt tweaks, most not. By end of Wednesday we had ~200 candidates that had passed first-pass review.

Thursday was the human review pass. The two designers walked the full set, removed 14 visuals that didn't hold up, generated 32 replacements, and shipped a v1 review pack to the client at 18:00 Thursday. The client came back Friday morning with 9 specific changes — all subtle copy or composition tweaks. We turned those by lunch.

04 FridayFinal delivery.

Friday afternoon was format export. 218 visuals across 10 formats — square, portrait, landscape, banner, story, carousel, plus print-ready variants for the regional adaptations. Auto-exported with format-specific composition tweaks (we'd built the export pipeline three weeks earlier on a different engagement). Delivered as a structured drive, with a one-page brief on usage.

The first thing the client said in the wrap call: 'We don't actually need to do this in the studio anymore, do we?' That's the question we want them to ask.
Creative lead · Visual

05 What workedThree things.

Modular prompt scaffolding — built once, reused across all 200 visuals. Changing locale was a parameter swap, not a re-prompting exercise.

Brand-specific LoRA — we'd trained a small fine-tune on this client's prior brand work in a previous engagement. The new work landed on-brand by default rather than after iteration.

Tight human review — automated QA caught colour and composition issues, but humans caught the things that needed taste. Both layers were necessary; neither alone would have shipped at this pace.

06 What we'd refineTwo things.

Faces. The hero visuals featured stylised people; we still struggle with consistent character identity across a full set. We worked around it by avoiding repeat characters across visuals. Next iteration: a small character LoRA per launch.

Print-ready output. The print export pipeline added two hours to Friday. We've since rebuilt that to run during generation, not after. Next time, no Friday afternoon spike.

07 The economicsWhat this cost.

Total engagement: USD 18k, four people for one week. The client's prior comparable shoot ran six weeks and roughly USD 90k. Output volume was higher this time. The savings aren't the headline — the speed is. Six weeks compressed into one means a launch can move with market signal rather than against a frozen plan.

We don't think AI replaces shoots. We think AI replaces certain kinds of shoots, the ones that were never about the photography in the first place but about the volume and the speed. This was one of those.
