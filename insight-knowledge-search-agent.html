Eight weeks to ship a knowledge-search AI agent.

From spec to operational, an AI agent built for a JP manufacturer's internal docs. The estimates we hit, the ones we missed, and the three details we'd refine next time.

Engineering xR&D Build · Da Nang
28 Apr 2026
Issue 008

A mid-sized JP manufacturer asked us to build a knowledge-search agent over twenty years of internal documentation. The brief was unfussy — make the docs answer questions in natural language. We quoted eight weeks. We shipped in eight weeks. About 70% of the work was where we expected it to be.

The remaining 30% was not. This is the build log: where the estimate held, where it didn't, and what we'd put on the spec sheet if we ran the same engagement again next quarter.

01 BriefWhat the client actually asked for.

The original ask was a chatbot. After two scoping sessions with the operations team, the actual ask was something narrower and more useful: a search interface that returns paragraph-length, source-linked answers from a heterogeneous corpus — PDFs scanned in 2008, mid-2010s confluence dumps, structured CMMS records, and a folder of engineering memos saved as PowerPoint.

  • Eight years of CMMS records (structured, ~120k entries)
  • Four years of confluence wiki (semi-structured, ~9k pages)
  • Twenty years of scanned engineering documents (unstructured, ~28k PDFs)
  • Operating notes in PowerPoint format that nobody had touched since 2017

02 ArchitectureBoring on purpose.

The architecture is unremarkable, which is the point. Document ingestion runs nightly, pushing chunks into a vector store. A retrieval layer pulls top-K candidates with a re-ranker pass, and a reasoning model composes the final answer with inline source citations. We benchmarked four embedding models and three rerankers on the client's actual queries before committing.

What we did differently was an explicit uncertainty layer. The agent is allowed to say "I don't have a confident answer" — and roughly 11% of the time, on real queries, that's exactly what it says. The client's first reaction was disappointment. Six weeks of operation later, it's the feature they cite most often as a trust signal.

03 Where we hit estimateThe boring 70%.

Ingestion, embedding, retrieval, ranking, response composition — all of it tracked. Our internal estimate was four developer-weeks for the core pipeline; we shipped at four-and-a-half. The OCR pass on the scanned PDFs ran twice as fast as we'd planned (vendor model improvements between quote and start), bought us two days, which we burned on something else.

04 Where we missedThree surprises.

Surprise one: the PowerPoint corpus. We'd estimated a day to extract text. Took four. The slides were heavily figure-driven, with text rendered as bitmap on a third of the deck. We ended up with a custom OCR-and-VLM pass and quietly billed it as "ingestion polish."

If a corpus has been touched by humans for twenty years, every weird ingestion edge case has been waiting for you, lined up.
Engineering lead · Build

Surprise two: Japanese-English mixed queries. The corpus is in Japanese; the operators sometimes type in English. The reranker we'd benchmarked on JP-only queries underperformed on the mixed set by a wide margin. We swapped models in week six. Two days lost, then recovered through better indexing.

Surprise three: the legal review took longer than the build. The client's compliance team needed to approve every category of source the agent could cite. Reasonable in hindsight. Unbudgeted on our side. Two weeks of calendar time, fortunately overlapped with QA.

05 Operational dataWhat it does, six weeks in.

The agent has been live for forty-three working days at the time of writing. Daily query volume has stabilised around 380. Answer satisfaction (thumbs-up rate) sits at 78% for confident answers, with the remaining queries split between "I don't have an answer" (11%) and explicit thumbs-down (about 11%). The thumbs-down cases are reviewed weekly; we ship retrieval tweaks every Friday.

06 What we'd refineThe next version.

Three refinements for v2, all already in the backlog:

  • Conversational follow-up. Single-shot queries cover most of the use, but the operators are starting to ask follow-ups. We'll ship session memory in the next iteration.
  • Inline figure retrieval. The agent answers in text. About 14% of the corpus value is in diagrams. v2 returns the relevant figure alongside the textual answer.
  • Active learning loop. Thumbs-down cases currently feed a manual review queue. v2 closes the loop automatically — bad answers retrain the reranker, with a human review gate before the model ships.

07 What we'd quoteFor the next client.

If we ran the same engagement again, the quote would be eight weeks for build, plus two weeks for compliance, plus a week of buffer for the corpus surprise we know is in there somewhere. So eleven weeks, calendared as eight build + three slack. We'd hold the price flat — the surprises are part of the work, not extras.

The bigger lesson is that knowledge-search agents are now a known shape of engagement for us. The architecture is stable, the team has done it before, and the surprises are starting to repeat. That's when an engagement type becomes a service offering.
