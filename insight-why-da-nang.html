Why we chose Da Nang.
Beyond the partner university.

The talent pipeline is the public reason. The deeper reasons — pace of life, room to think, distance from the noise — are the ones that actually shape the work.

Tomoki Founder · CEO
22 Mar 2026
Issue 002

Whenever someone asks why we chose Da Nang, I give them the public answer: the partner university, the talent pipeline, the cost of operating in central Vietnam. All true. None of it is the actual reason. The actual reason is harder to put on a slide, so I'm putting it here.

01 The public reasonThe partner university.

Yes, the partner university is in Da Nang. Yes, the pipeline runs through it. Yes, that pipeline is the operational reason a Tokyo-based founder ended up running a Vietnamese studio. If we'd had to pick a city in Vietnam without that anchor, the answer might have been Hanoi or Ho Chi Minh City. The university decided the geography.

This is the answer I give clients, partners, and anyone who needs a thirty-second story. It's complete enough not to need follow-up. It's also incomplete in ways that matter.

02 Why not TokyoThe noise.

I lived in Tokyo for the eight years before this. It is the most concentrated software founder ecosystem in Japan. It is also extremely loud. There is a meetup every night, a pitch event every weekend, a friend-of-a-friend's startup launch every Tuesday morning. All of it generates real opportunity, and all of it generates a kind of attention tax that, in retrospect, I was paying without noticing.

The first six months in Da Nang, I noticed I was thinking differently. Not better, not faster — quieter. The space between problems felt longer. Decisions that would have taken a week of conversations in Tokyo took a long walk and a notebook. The work got slower in a way that I now think is correct.

03 Why not Hanoi or HCMCThe scale.

The other Vietnamese options have more obvious infrastructure. Hanoi has the JP-side networks, HCMC has the venture capital. Both have larger software ecosystems. Both also have more of the noise that Tokyo has — bigger ecosystems mean more meetings, more events, more reasons to optimise for visibility over depth.

Da Nang is a small city by Vietnamese standards. There is no software founder dinner circuit. There is no startup conference. There are about four other AI-native teams in town that we know personally, and we see them irregularly rather than constantly. That suits us.

04 The paceMornings, walks, the river.

The studio's working day starts at 09:30 and ends at 18:30 with a generous lunch in the middle. Before work, most of the team walks or runs by the river. After work, the courtyard fills up with whoever is around. None of this would happen in Tokyo or HCMC — the commutes are too long, the days are too compressed, the city itself doesn't permit it.

The pace of the city sets the pace of the work. We didn't fully understand that until we'd been here a year.
Tomoki · Founder

I'd argue this is the single biggest non-public reason we work the way we do. The work happens slowly enough to think; the team has lives outside the studio; the calendar isn't packed by default. None of this would survive in a different city.

05 The distanceFar enough to be useful.

Tokyo is a five-hour direct flight. We do the trip roughly monthly. Distance is enough to keep us out of the day-to-day in JP — which is good for our clients, who don't need us to be in the room — and close enough to fly in for a critical meeting on a day's notice. The time difference is two hours, manageable for most working calendars.

If we were in Tokyo, the temptation to attend everything would be too strong. If we were in Singapore or Bangkok, we'd be too far to drop in. Da Nang's distance from JP turns out to be calibrated almost perfectly.

06 The harder bitWhat it costs.

The cost is real. We are not in the room when JP networks meet. We miss conferences. JP-side hires are harder — most senior JP engineers don't want to relocate, and the few who do are unusual enough that they self-select for the work. Press coverage in JP requires more deliberate effort than it would for a Tokyo-based studio.

We accept these costs. The trade was deliberate, even before we fully understood what we were trading for. The work the city permits is worth more to us than the work we miss by not being in Tokyo.

07 What's nextStaying here.

We considered a Tokyo office at one point. We may still open a small one — for client meetings, JP-hire onboarding, the occasional founder dinner. But the centre of gravity will stay in Da Nang for as long as it serves the work. There is no universe where we move HQ to Tokyo.

The full long-form on this — what we've learned about working from a smaller city, what other founders should think about before doing the same — ships in the autumn dossier. This is the short version. If you're considering similar moves, drop a line to the studio. We've made enough mistakes to be useful.
